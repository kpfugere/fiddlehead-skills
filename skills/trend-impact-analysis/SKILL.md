# Trend → Impact Analysis

This skill takes an emerging trend and plays out the consequences: if this trend continues on its current trajectory, what happens when it crosses key thresholds? Who wins, who loses, what breaks, what gets created, and what does it mean?

The goal is not to predict the future with certainty. It is to think rigorously about a plausible future so that leaders can position themselves now, and to interpret that future with the kind of depth that separates a strategy memo from a shrug.

## Style Note: No Em Dashes

Do not use em dashes (—) anywhere in the output. Use commas, periods, colons, semicolons, or parentheses instead. This applies to every section of every analysis produced by this skill.

## The Core Exercise

The analysis follows a simple but powerful chain:

**Trend → Milestone → Imagined World → Disruption Map → Philosophical Interpretation → Strategic Implications**

1. **Understand the trend.** What is actually happening? Ground it in data.
2. **Identify milestones.** What adoption thresholds or inflection points matter?
3. **Imagine the world at each milestone.** What does daily life, business, and competition look like once this milestone is reached?
4. **Map the disruptions.** Who and what gets displaced, transformed, or created?
5. **Interpret through a philosophical lens.** What do thinkers say this shift means for humans, power, meaning, and value?
6. **Extract strategic implications.** What should a leader do now given this analysis?

## Step 1: Research the Trend

Before imagining anything, build a factual foundation. Use web search to gather:

- **Current state**: Where is this trend today? What are the latest adoption numbers, market size, growth rates?
- **Trajectory**: What is the rate of change? Is it accelerating, linear, or plateauing?
- **Key players**: Who is driving this trend? Who is investing heavily?
- **Barriers**: What could slow or stop it? Regulation, technical limits, cost curves?
- **Analogies**: Has anything similar happened before? What can we learn from those historical parallels?

Be specific with numbers. "AI adoption is growing fast" is useless. "Enterprise AI spending grew 35% YoY to $XX billion in 2025, with 72% of Fortune 500 companies now running at least one production LLM workload" is useful.

## Step 2: Define the Milestones

Milestones are the thresholds where the trend starts changing the game. These are typically adoption percentages, cost crossover points, or capability thresholds.

For each milestone, define:
- **What it is**: e.g., "50% of US households have a smart speaker"
- **When it might happen**: Based on current trajectory
- **Why it matters**: What behavior or market shift does this threshold unlock?

Think about milestones the way the smartphone example illustrates (see the reference section below). Smartphone adoption at 25% meant apps were a viable business. At 50%, mobile-first design became mandatory. At 75%, entire industries could assume a smartphone in every pocket, which unlocked ride-sharing, mobile payments, and the death of paper maps.

The user may specify how many milestones to explore, or you can suggest 2-3 that represent meaningfully different worlds.

## Step 3: Imagine the World

This is the creative heart of the exercise. For each milestone, paint a vivid picture of what the world looks like. Do not just list abstract changes. Describe concrete shifts in behavior, business models, and power dynamics.

Ask yourself:
- **What becomes possible** that is not possible today?
- **What becomes unnecessary** that people currently rely on?
- **What becomes the default assumption** that today is still a question?
- **Who has new power** they did not have before?
- **What daily behaviors change** for ordinary people, workers, and executives?

The smartphone at 75% penetration did not just mean "more people have phones." It meant: every restaurant needs an online ordering system. Cash is optional. Your taxi company is dead unless you have an app. Physical newspapers are a niche product. Every 16-year-old has a camera, a video studio, and a publishing platform in their pocket. The mall is competing with a device on the couch.

Push yourself to be concrete and vivid. Abstract statements like "business models will change" do not help anyone make decisions.

## Step 4: Map the Disruptions

Now get systematic. For the imagined world at each milestone, identify:

### Who/What Gets Disrupted
- **Incumbents at risk**: Which established companies or industries are most exposed? Why?
- **Business models that break**: Which revenue models, cost structures, or value propositions stop working?
- **Skills that depreciate**: What expertise becomes less valuable?
- **Assumptions that shatter**: What "common knowledge" turns out to be wrong?

### Who/What Gets Created or Elevated
- **New markets**: What categories of product or service emerge?
- **New power brokers**: Who gains disproportionate influence?
- **New skills in demand**: What capabilities become critical?
- **New dependencies**: What infrastructure or platforms become essential?

### The Transition Zone
- **Winners who adapt**: Which incumbents could actually benefit if they move fast?
- **Hybrid models**: What transitional business models bridge old and new?
- **Regulatory flashpoints**: Where will regulation lag, and what chaos results?

## Step 5: Philosophical Interpretation

Strategy without interpretation is just spreadsheet fatalism. This step asks what the shift *means*: for human agency, power, meaning, value, and the shape of the social world. Executives do not need a philosophy seminar, but they do need a deeper read than "the market is changing."

### How to Use the Roster

Select **2 to 3 philosophers from the roster below** whose frameworks most illuminate the specific trend being analyzed. Do not apply all ten. Choose the ones whose questions cut closest to what is actually at stake in this particular shift. For each selected philosopher, write a short paragraph (3-6 sentences) that:

1. Names the philosopher and the one concept most relevant to this trend.
2. Applies that concept directly to the imagined world and disruption map from earlier steps.
3. Surfaces a tension, risk, or opportunity that a purely strategic read would miss.

Close the section with a **synthesis paragraph** that ties the philosophical readings together into a single interpretive claim about what this trend means.

### The Roster

- **Yuval Noah Harari** (narrative and meaning): Strong on how stories, myths, and shared fictions shape economies and institutions. Ask: what new story does this trend force us to tell about humans, work, or value? What old story stops being believable?
- **Shoshana Zuboff** (surveillance capitalism, behavioral extraction): Strong on trends that turn human behavior into predictive product. Ask: who is extracting what from whom, and what asymmetries of knowledge and power result?
- **Byung-Chul Han** (burnout society, disappearance of the other): Strong on the felt, phenomenological texture of modern life. Ask: does this trend deepen self-exploitation, frictionless consumption, or the erosion of genuine encounter?
- **Michael Sandel** (moral limits of markets): Strong on trends that expand market logic into domains that used to be governed by other norms. Ask: what is being commodified here that resists commodification, and what civic or moral goods get crowded out?
- **Martha Nussbaum** (capabilities approach): Strong on trends with distributional and human-flourishing stakes. Ask: does this expand or contract real human capabilities (health, bodily integrity, affiliation, play, practical reason) across populations?
- **Peter Singer** (utilitarian ethics, expanding moral circle): Strong on trends that touch suffering, animals, or global welfare. Ask: what aggregate suffering is reduced or produced, and whose interests are we finally forced to count?
- **Nick Bostrom** (existential risk, long-term thinking): Strong on trends with tail risks or civilizational implications. Ask: what low-probability, high-magnitude outcomes become plausible, and what does the long now demand of us?
- **Luciano Floridi** (philosophy of information, the infosphere): Strong on trends that reshape what counts as real, agent, or environment. Ask: how does this blur the human/digital boundary, and what new ontology do we inherit?
- **Donna Haraway** (cyborgs, staying with the trouble, dissolving dualisms): Strong on trends that scramble categories (human/machine, natural/artificial, organic/engineered). Ask: which old dualisms stop working, and what hybrid forms and kinships emerge?
- **Charles Taylor** (social imaginaries, authenticity): Strong on how shared background understandings of "how things are" shift under technological change. Ask: what new social imaginary is being constructed, and what does it quietly make thinkable or unthinkable?

### Guidance for Choosing

A rough mapping for common trend types, not a rule:

- **AI, automation, knowledge work**: Zuboff, Han, Harari, Floridi
- **Crypto, payments, monetary change**: Sandel, Harari, Zuboff
- **Biotech, food, bodies**: Singer, Haraway, Nussbaum, Sandel
- **Mobility, autonomous systems**: Taylor, Harari, Bostrom
- **Climate, energy, resources**: Nussbaum, Bostrom, Haraway
- **Platforms, attention, media**: Zuboff, Han, Taylor

Pick by relevance, not category. A single trend may pull a philosopher from outside its usual column.

## Step 6: Strategic Implications

Close with actionable takeaways. The depth here depends on who the analysis is for.

**For executives/C-suite** (the default unless the user specifies otherwise):
- 3 to 5 crisp "so what" statements
- Decisions that should be made in the next 6 to 12 months
- Bets to place, hedges to make
- What to monitor as leading indicators

**For product/strategy teams** (if the user requests more detail):
- Specific product opportunities and threats
- Build vs. buy vs. partner decisions
- Talent and capability gaps to close
- Competitive moves to watch

**For broader audiences**:
- Adjust tone and jargon level to match the user's context

Where possible, let the philosophical interpretation from Step 5 sharpen the strategic implications. If Zuboff's lens surfaced an extraction asymmetry, one strategic move should address that directly. If Sandel flagged a commodification risk, a hedge should exist for the backlash.

## Configuring Depth

The user can specify how deep the impact chain should go. By default, aim for 3 levels:

- **Level 1**: Direct market impact (who competes with this trend directly)
- **Level 2**: Secondary industry effects (adjacent industries that feel the ripple)
- **Level 3**: Behavioral and structural shifts (how society, regulation, or culture adapts)

If the user asks for deeper analysis (5+ levels), continue the chain into:

- **Level 4**: Counter-reactions and backlash (regulatory response, consumer resistance, ethical debates)
- **Level 5**: Long-term equilibrium (what the "new normal" looks like once the dust settles)

## Output Format

The user chooses the format. If they do not specify, default to a structured markdown report. Options:

- **Markdown report**: Sections for each step above, with a concise executive summary up top
- **Slide deck**: If the user asks for a presentation, use the pptx skill to create a deck
- **Conversational**: If the user wants a discussion, walk through the analysis step by step in conversation

Regardless of format, always lead with the punchline, the single most important insight, before walking through the supporting analysis. And remember: no em dashes anywhere in the final output.

## Reference: The Smartphone Calibration

This historical example illustrates how the exercise should work. When someone in 2007-2010 asked "what happens if smartphone adoption continues at this pace?", here is what playing it forward would have revealed:

**The Trend (circa 2008-2010):** iPhone launched 2007. App Store opened 2008. Android growing fast. Smartphones going from ~15% to ~25% US penetration.

**Milestone 1, 25% penetration (~2010):**
Apps become a viable business. The "app economy" is born. Mobile advertising starts to matter. Blackberry is still dominant in enterprise but the writing is on the wall.

**Milestone 2, 50% penetration (~2012):**
Mobile-first design becomes mandatory for any consumer business. E-commerce shifts to mobile. Social media goes mobile-native (Instagram, Snapchat emerge). Feature phones start disappearing from carrier shelves. GPS-enabled services explode.

**Milestone 3, 75% penetration (~2014-2015):**
Now you can build businesses that *assume* everyone has a smartphone. This is when the real disruption hits:
- **Ride-sharing** (Uber, Lyft): impossible without ubiquitous GPS-equipped smartphones
- **Mobile payments**: cash and cards face a credible challenger
- **Gig economy**: TaskRabbit, DoorDash, Instacart all need phones on both sides
- **Traditional retail**: showrooming becomes standard, Amazon becomes the default
- **Media**: cord-cutting accelerates, YouTube/Netflix on every commute, print media collapses
- **Banking**: branch visits plummet, mobile-only banks emerge
- **Navigation**: Garmin's standalone GPS business devastated, paper maps extinct
- **Photography**: point-and-shoot camera market collapses, everyone is a photographer
- **Local business**: Yelp reviews and Google Maps become make-or-break for restaurants

**What this example teaches us about the exercise:**
The biggest disruptions came not from the phone itself but from what became possible when you could *assume* everyone had one. That is the key insight to replicate: trend analysis is not about the trend in isolation. It is about the ecosystem of possibilities that emerge at each milestone.

Use this pattern, but do not just copy it. Every trend has its own unique cascade. The smartphone example is here to calibrate your thinking about *which figures get disrupted* and *at what level of abstraction to operate*.
