# Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) — Reference

## What It Is
A framework that reframes strategy around **what customers are actually trying to accomplish** rather than product features or demographics. Core premise: people "hire" products to get a "job" done. Understanding the job reveals the real competitive landscape and uncovers innovation opportunities that feature-centric thinking misses.

*"People don't want to buy a quarter-inch drill. They want a quarter-inch hole."* — Theodore Levitt

## Key Concepts

### What Is a "Job"?
A job is the **fundamental goal or progress** a customer seeks in a given situation. It's defined independently of any product or solution.

**Jobs are NOT:** features, tasks, or product use cases.
**Jobs ARE:** the higher-level outcome the customer wants, described solution-agnostically.

*Example: The job isn't "use a taxi app" — it's "get across town quickly and reliably so I arrive on time."*

Jobs remain **stable over time** even as solutions evolve. People have always needed to "get across town" — the means changed from horses to cars to rideshare apps.

### Three Dimensions of Every Job

**Functional** — The practical task or problem to solve.
*"Get from A to B quickly."*

**Emotional** — How the customer wants to feel during/after.
*"Feel safe, calm, and in control of my schedule."*

**Social** — How the customer wants to be perceived.
*"Appear responsible and punctual to colleagues."*

Winning products address all three dimensions. Understanding the emotional and social layers often explains why customers choose one solution over a functionally equivalent alternative.

### Job Stories (Format)
**"When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [desired outcome]."**

*"When I'm rushing to work in the morning, I want a breakfast I can eat in the car, so I can arrive feeling satisfied and focused."*

This format captures context (when), motivation (want), and outcome (so). It deliberately omits personas and specific solutions.

### Uncovering Unmet Needs
The biggest innovation opportunities live where jobs are **high importance but low satisfaction** with current solutions.

**Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI)** process:
1. Map the job into steps (the Job Map — see below)
2. For each step, identify outcome statements ("minimize the time to ___", "reduce the likelihood of ___")
3. Survey customers on importance and satisfaction for each outcome
4. High importance + low satisfaction = underserved opportunity

### The Job Map
A step-by-step breakdown of how customers complete a job (independent of any product):

1. **Define** — Determine objectives and plan
2. **Locate** — Gather needed resources/information
3. **Prepare** — Set up and organize inputs
4. **Confirm** — Verify readiness
5. **Execute** — Carry out the core task
6. **Monitor** — Check progress
7. **Modify** — Make adjustments
8. **Conclude** — Finish and wrap up

Map these steps for the customer's job. Then identify where friction, workarounds, or dissatisfaction exists at each step. That's where innovation should focus.

### Unexpected Competition
JTBD reveals that your real competitors are **anything the customer could hire for the same job** — not just similar products.

*Classic example: Morning milkshakes compete with bananas, bagels, and coffee — anything hired for the job of "keep me full and occupied during my commute."*

## How to Apply It

1. **Identify the core job.** Interview customers. Ask "what were you trying to accomplish?" not "what features do you want?" Look for the progress they seek, not the product they use.
2. **Map the full job.** Use the 8-step Job Map to see the complete picture. Most companies only serve part of the job — the unserved steps are opportunities.
3. **Capture all three dimensions.** Don't stop at functional. Ask "how did you want to feel?" and "how did you want others to see you?" The emotional and social layers drive differentiation.
4. **Find unmet needs.** Look for high-importance, low-satisfaction outcomes. These are innovation gold.
5. **Redefine the competitive set.** Ask "what else could customers hire for this job?" This often reveals surprising alternatives and threat vectors.
6. **Design solutions around the job.** Features, messaging, and pricing should all map back to how well they get the job done.

## Common Pitfalls
- Defining jobs too narrowly (describing a task with your product, not the outcome)
- Defining jobs too broadly (so general it doesn't guide decisions)
- Skipping emotional/social dimensions — functional analysis alone misses why people really choose
- Confusing "jobs" with "needs" expressed as feature requests — dig deeper
- Assuming the current product category IS the job — look wider

## When to Combine With Other Frameworks
- **+ Blue Ocean**: JTBD reveals the underserved jobs; Blue Ocean designs the offering to own that space.
- **+ Business Model Canvas**: JTBD sharpens the Value Proposition and Customer Segments blocks.
- **+ Golden Circle**: JTBD answers "what job do customers hire us for?" — which informs the company's Why.
- **+ Core Competencies**: Are our core strengths aligned with the jobs that matter most to customers?
