# Porter's Five Forces — Reference

## What It Is
Michael Porter's framework for analyzing **industry-level competitive dynamics**. It determines whether an industry is structurally attractive (profitable) or unattractive by examining five forces that shape competition. The stronger the forces, the harder it is for firms in that industry to earn above-average returns.

## The Five Forces

### 1. Threat of New Entrants
Can new competitors easily enter this industry?

**High threat when:** Low capital requirements, weak brand loyalty, easy distribution access, few regulations, low switching costs, no scale advantages.
**Low threat when:** High capital investment needed, strong incumbents with brand loyalty, regulatory barriers, patents/IP, significant economies of scale, high switching costs.

*Example: Restaurant industry = high threat (low barriers). Semiconductor manufacturing = low threat (massive capital, IP, expertise required).*

### 2. Bargaining Power of Suppliers
Can suppliers squeeze the industry's margins?

**High power when:** Few suppliers dominate, inputs are differentiated or unique, high switching costs, suppliers can forward-integrate, no substitutes for their inputs.
**Low power when:** Many suppliers compete, inputs are commoditized, easy to switch, buyers can backward-integrate.

*Example: Intel's power over PC manufacturers (few alternatives for x86 chips) vs. commodity steel suppliers (many interchangeable sources).*

### 3. Bargaining Power of Buyers
Can customers force prices down or demand more?

**High power when:** Few large buyers, standardized/undifferentiated products, low switching costs, price-sensitive buyers, buyers can backward-integrate, full price transparency.
**Low power when:** Fragmented buyers, differentiated products, high switching costs, brand loyalty, information asymmetry.

*Example: Walmart's power over consumer goods suppliers vs. individual consumers buying luxury goods.*

### 4. Threat of Substitutes
Can customers switch to fundamentally different solutions?

**High threat when:** Substitutes offer better price-performance, low switching costs, buyers willing to try alternatives, substitutes from outside the traditional industry.
**Low threat when:** No close substitutes exist, high switching costs, substitutes are inferior on key dimensions.

*Example: Video streaming as substitute for cable TV. Email/Slack as substitute for business travel meetings.*

### 5. Rivalry Among Existing Competitors
How fiercely do current players compete?

**High rivalry when:** Many competitors of similar size, slow industry growth, high fixed costs (pressure to fill capacity), low differentiation, high exit barriers.
**Low rivalry when:** Few dominant players, fast-growing market, differentiated products, low fixed costs, rational competitors.

*Example: Airlines (high rivalry — similar offerings, price-sensitive customers, high fixed costs) vs. luxury watchmaking (lower rivalry — differentiated, brand-loyal, less price competition).*

## How to Apply It

1. **Define the industry boundaries** clearly. Too broad (e.g., "technology") = meaningless. Too narrow (e.g., "red sneakers for left-handed people") = useless.
2. **Assess each force** as High / Medium / Low. For each, identify the 2-3 most important factors driving its strength.
3. **Determine overall industry attractiveness.** If 3+ forces are strong, the industry is structurally challenging. If most are weak, it's attractive.
4. **Identify strategic implications:**
   - Which forces most threaten profitability?
   - Can you position to reduce exposure to the strongest forces?
   - Can you exploit weak forces (e.g., high buyer power → differentiate to reduce it)?
5. **Look at trends.** Forces change over time. Is supplier power increasing? Are new substitutes emerging? This dynamic view is where the real strategic insight lives.

## Common Pitfalls
- Confusing industry analysis with company analysis — Five Forces is about the industry, not one firm
- Ignoring the "substitute" force — the most disruptive threats often come from outside the traditional industry
- Treating forces as static — always consider how they're shifting
- Forgetting that the goal is strategic positioning, not just description

## When to Combine With Other Frameworks
- **+ SWOT**: Porter's shows the external landscape; SWOT adds the internal dimension. The canonical pairing.
- **+ Blue Ocean**: Porter's reveals the red ocean dynamics; Blue Ocean shows how to escape them.
- **+ BCG Matrix**: Porter's assesses industry attractiveness for each business unit; BCG maps portfolio positioning.
