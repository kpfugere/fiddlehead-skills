# BCG Growth-Share Matrix — Reference

## What It Is
A 2x2 portfolio management tool from Boston Consulting Group. Plots business units or products on two axes — **market growth rate** (vertical) and **relative market share** (horizontal) — to guide resource allocation decisions. The core insight: use cash from mature winners to fund future growth.

## The Four Quadrants

### Stars (High Growth + High Share)
Growth drivers. Strong position in a fast-growing market. Generate significant revenue but consume heavy investment to maintain leadership. Strategy: **invest aggressively** to hold or build position. Successful Stars become Cash Cows as markets mature.
*Example: Tesla EVs in early 2010s — dominant share in a booming market.*

### Cash Cows (Low Growth + High Share)
Profit engines. Dominant position in a mature, slow-growth market. Generate far more cash than needed to maintain. Strategy: **harvest/milk** — invest just enough to sustain, redirect surplus to Stars and Question Marks.
*Example: Microsoft Office, Coca-Cola Classic — massive cash generators in stable markets.*

### Question Marks (High Growth + Low Share)
Strategic dilemmas. Small share in a fast-growing market. Require significant investment to compete but uncertain payoff. Strategy: **selectively invest** in the most promising ones (aim to turn into Stars), divest the rest.
*Example: Early Uber Eats — booming market but small share vs. DoorDash.*

### Dogs (Low Growth + Low Share)
Underperformers. Small share in a stagnant market. Generate minimal cash and tie up resources. Strategy: **divest, phase out, or harvest** — unless strategically important for other reasons (bundling, customer retention, synergies).
*Example: BlackBerry smartphones by mid-2010s, Apple iPod in its final years.*

## How to Apply It

1. **List all business units or product lines** to evaluate.
2. **Determine market growth rate** for each unit's market. Use industry data. Define a threshold for "high" vs. "low" (often ~10%, but varies by industry).
3. **Calculate relative market share** = Your share ÷ Largest competitor's share. Above 1.0 = high (you're the leader). Below 1.0 = low.
4. **Plot each unit** on the matrix. Optionally size bubbles by revenue.
5. **Assess portfolio balance:**
   - Healthy: Mix of Cash Cows (funding today) + Stars (growth) + selective Question Marks (future bets), few Dogs.
   - Unhealthy: All Dogs/Question Marks (no cash engine), or all Cash Cows (no growth pipeline).
6. **Assign strategies** per quadrant and plan resource flows (Cows → Stars/Question Marks).

## Key Dynamics
Products migrate through quadrants over time:
- **Ideal path**: Question Mark → Star → Cash Cow (gained share, market matured)
- **Failure path**: Question Mark → Dog (never gained share, market slowed)
- **Decline path**: Cash Cow → Dog (lost share as market shifted)

Effective portfolio management means actively managing this lifecycle.

## Common Pitfalls
- Only two dimensions — ignores profitability, synergies, competitive dynamics, strategic importance
- Assumes high share = high profitability (not always true)
- Can encourage premature divestiture of "Dogs" that have strategic value
- Market definition matters enormously — the same product can be a Star or Dog depending on how you scope the market
- Snapshot in time — always pair with dynamic/forward-looking analysis

## When to Combine With Other Frameworks
- **+ Three Horizons**: BCG shows current portfolio state; Three Horizons adds the time dimension for future planning.
- **+ Core Competencies**: Helps decide which Question Marks to bet on — those aligned with core strengths.
- **+ Porter's Five Forces**: Assesses whether a Star's industry is actually attractive (high-share in a structurally unattractive industry isn't as good as it looks).
