# Core Competencies — Reference

## What It Is
A framework (from Prahalad & Hamel, 1990) for identifying an organization's **unique, hard-to-replicate strengths** that drive competitive advantage. Takes an "inside-out" view: instead of chasing every market opportunity, focus strategy on what you do better than anyone else and build from there.

## The Three Tests
A capability qualifies as a **core competency** only if it passes all three:

1. **Provides access to multiple markets.** It's not just useful for one product — it opens doors to diverse opportunities.
   *Honda's engine expertise → cars, motorcycles, lawn mowers, generators, boats.*

2. **Contributes significantly to customer value.** Customers recognize and value it — it's a key reason they choose you.
   *Apple's design excellence → customers pay premium prices because the experience matters to them.*

3. **Difficult for competitors to imitate.** It's built over time through unique combinations of skills, culture, processes, and knowledge.
   *3M's innovation culture → can't be copied by hiring a few engineers; it's embedded in decades of organizational DNA.*

If a capability only passes 1-2 tests, it's a **strength** but not a core competency. Most organizations have many capabilities but only 2-4 true core competencies.

## Resources → Capabilities → Core Competencies

- **Resources**: What you have (assets, people, IP, cash)
- **Capabilities**: What you can do (processes, skills applied to resources)
- **Core Competencies**: The few capabilities that are strategically unique and drive lasting advantage

## Visual Tools

### Venn Diagram
Three overlapping circles — one per test criterion. Only capabilities in the center (passing all three) are core competencies. Useful for workshops to filter a long list of "strengths" down to the real core.

### Competency-Market Matrix (2x2)
| | Existing Markets | New Markets |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Competencies** | **Fill in the Blanks** — Reinforce current strengths in current markets | **White Spaces** — Apply proven strengths to new markets |
| **New Competencies** | **Premier Plus Ten** — Develop new capabilities for current customers | **Mega Opportunities** — New capabilities + new markets (highest risk) |

Use this to map growth initiatives and ensure they're grounded in competency reality. The safest growth usually stays close to existing competencies.

## How to Apply It

1. **Audit capabilities.** List major capabilities across all functions. Interview executives, frontline staff, and customers. Ask: "What do we do better than anyone?" and "What would alarm our competitors if they knew?"
2. **Apply the three tests.** For each candidate, rigorously ask: Does it open multiple markets? Does it drive customer choice? Is it hard to copy? Be honest — most strengths won't pass all three.
3. **Identify 2-4 core competencies.** That's typical. If you have 10, you haven't been rigorous enough.
4. **Align strategy around them:**
   - **Investment**: Prioritize R&D, talent, and capital toward core areas
   - **Talent**: Hire and develop people who strengthen core competencies
   - **Product development**: New offerings should leverage core competencies
   - **Outsourcing**: Activities that aren't core → candidates for outsourcing or partnership
5. **Watch for core rigidities.** A competency that stops evolving can become a liability. Periodically reassess: "Is this still differentiating? Will it be in 5 years?"

## Classic Examples
- **Honda**: Engine design and manufacturing → spans autos, bikes, marine, power equipment
- **Apple**: Design integration and user experience → spans computers, phones, watches, services
- **3M**: Innovation culture and materials science → 50,000+ products across dozens of industries
- **Amazon**: Scalable IT infrastructure → led to AWS; logistics mastery → led to marketplace dominance
- **Walmart**: Supply chain and logistics → enables everyday low prices at massive scale

## Common Pitfalls
- Confusing "things we do" with "things only we can do well" — core competencies must be unique and hard to copy
- Listing too many — if everything is core, nothing is
- Ignoring competency decay — what was core 10 years ago may not be today
- Using core competencies as an excuse not to innovate — "that's not our competency" can block necessary evolution
- Forgetting the customer lens — a competency that doesn't contribute to customer value isn't strategically core

## When to Combine With Other Frameworks
- **+ SWOT**: Core Competencies deepens the "Strengths" analysis — which strengths are truly sustainable?
- **+ BCG Matrix**: Guides Question Mark decisions — invest in the ones aligned with core competencies.
- **+ Three Horizons**: Which competencies power H1? Which need development for H2/H3?
- **+ Business Model Canvas**: Core competencies should show up in Key Resources and Key Activities.
