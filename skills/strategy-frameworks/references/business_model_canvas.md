# Business Model Canvas — Reference

## What It Is
A one-page strategic tool (created by Alexander Osterwalder) that maps a business model across **9 interconnected building blocks**. It visualizes how a company creates, delivers, and captures value — making the whole system visible at a glance. Useful for startups, established companies, and anyone evaluating or designing a business model.

## The Nine Building Blocks

### Right Side (Market-Facing / Value Delivery)

**Customer Segments** — Who do you serve?
Define the specific groups of people or organizations you create value for. Can be mass market, niche, segmented, multi-sided (e.g., Airbnb serves both hosts and guests). Different segments may need different value propositions, channels, and relationships.

**Value Propositions** — Why do customers choose you?
The bundle of benefits that solve customer problems or satisfy needs. Can be based on: novelty, performance, customization, design, brand, price, cost reduction, risk reduction, accessibility, convenience. This is the CENTER of the canvas — the bridge between what you build and who you serve.

**Channels** — How do you reach customers?
Communication, distribution, and sales pathways. Direct (your own website, stores, sales team) or indirect (partners, retailers, app stores). Covers the full cycle: awareness → evaluation → purchase → delivery → after-sales.

**Customer Relationships** — What kind of relationship do you maintain?
Personal assistance, dedicated account management, self-service, automated services, communities, co-creation. Driven by customer acquisition, retention, and upselling goals.

**Revenue Streams** — How do you make money?
Asset sales, subscription fees, licensing, advertising, commissions/brokerage, usage fees, freemium, renting/leasing. Each segment may have different revenue streams.

### Left Side (Operational / Value Creation)

**Key Resources** — What assets are essential?
Physical (facilities, equipment), intellectual (IP, brands, data, algorithms), human (talent, expertise), financial (cash, credit). The critical assets that make the model work.

**Key Activities** — What must you do exceptionally well?
Production, problem-solving, platform/network management, R&D, marketing, supply chain. The core processes that deliver the value proposition.

**Key Partnerships** — Who helps you?
Strategic alliances, joint ventures, buyer-supplier relationships, coopetition. Reasons: acquire resources/activities you can't do alone, reduce risk, gain economies of scale.

**Cost Structure** — What are the major costs?
Fixed costs (salaries, rent), variable costs (materials, usage-based fees). Is the model cost-driven (lean, low-price) or value-driven (premium, experience-focused)? Map major cost categories to key resources, activities, and partnerships.

## How to Apply It

1. **Start with Customer Segments + Value Propositions.** Nail the product-market fit first. For each segment, articulate a clear value proposition.
2. **Map the right side.** How do you reach these customers (Channels)? What relationship do you maintain? How do they pay (Revenue Streams)?
3. **Map the left side.** What resources, activities, and partners are needed to deliver the value proposition?
4. **Check Cost Structure.** Can revenue streams cover costs? Where are the biggest cost drivers?
5. **Do a coherence check.** Walk through the narrative: "We serve [segments] with [value prop], delivered through [channels], maintaining [relationships], earning [revenue]. To do this, we need [resources], performing [activities], with help from [partners], costing [costs]." Any gaps?
6. **Iterate.** Use sticky notes (physical or virtual). Swap elements, test alternatives, compare before/after for pivots.

## Analysis Tips
- **Look for misalignments**: A customer segment with no revenue stream. A key activity with no supporting resource. A value proposition that channels can't deliver.
- **Compare models**: Draw competitor BMCs side by side to spot differentiation opportunities.
- **Test pivots**: Change one block and trace the ripple effects through others.
- **Multi-sided platforms**: Draw the canvas addressing each side (e.g., for a marketplace: what's the value prop for sellers vs. buyers?).

## Common Pitfalls
- Cramming too much detail — keep it high-level (that's the point of a canvas)
- Treating it as static — it's a living tool, revise as you learn
- Ignoring the connections between blocks — the value is in the system, not individual boxes
- Confusing value propositions with features — VP is about the *benefit* to the customer, not specs

## When to Combine With Other Frameworks
- **+ JTBD**: Deep-dive into Value Propositions and Customer Segments — what job does each segment hire you for?
- **+ Blue Ocean**: After ERRC analysis, map the new offering onto BMC to validate it's operationally viable.
- **+ SWOT**: BMC maps the system; SWOT evaluates its strengths and vulnerabilities.
- **+ Porter's Five Forces**: BMC shows your model; Porter's shows whether the industry structure supports it.
