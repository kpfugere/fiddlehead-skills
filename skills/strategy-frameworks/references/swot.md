# SWOT Analysis — Reference

## What It Is
A 2x2 matrix assessing **Strengths** (internal +), **Weaknesses** (internal −), **Opportunities** (external +), and **Threats** (external −). The most widely used strategic diagnostic — fast, versatile, and a natural starting point for any analysis.

## The Four Quadrants

**Strengths** — Internal capabilities and advantages over competitors:
Brand reputation, proprietary technology, skilled workforce, financial resources, loyal customers, efficient processes, IP/patents, strong culture, market share leadership.

**Weaknesses** — Internal limitations and vulnerabilities:
Skill gaps, high debt, outdated systems, poor brand perception, operational inefficiency, high turnover, limited R&D, narrow product line, weak distribution.

**Opportunities** — External trends and openings to exploit:
Emerging markets, new technologies, regulatory shifts, competitor weaknesses, changing consumer behavior, partnership possibilities, demographic trends, industry consolidation.

**Threats** — External risks and headwinds:
New competitors, substitutes, economic downturns, regulatory tightening, supply chain disruption, shifting customer preferences, price wars, technology obsolescence.

## How to Apply It

1. **Define the scope.** What are you analyzing? A whole company, a product line, a market entry decision? Narrow focus = better results.
2. **Gather diverse input.** Pull from multiple perspectives — sales, product, ops, finance, customers. Avoid groupthink.
3. **Be specific and evidence-based.** "Strong brand" is vague. "85% brand awareness among target demo (per Q3 survey)" is useful. Quantify where possible.
4. **List 3-7 items per quadrant.** Enough to be thorough, not so many that nothing stands out.
5. **Prioritize.** Rank by impact. Not all strengths are equally important. Highlight the 1-2 factors per quadrant that matter most.
6. **Cross-reference quadrants for strategy:**
   - **SO strategies**: Use strengths to seize opportunities
   - **WO strategies**: Address weaknesses to unlock opportunities
   - **ST strategies**: Use strengths to counter threats
   - **WT strategies**: Minimize weaknesses to reduce exposure to threats

## Common Pitfalls
- Listing vague platitudes instead of specific, evidenced factors
- Confusing internal weaknesses with external threats (if you control it, it's internal)
- Ignoring uncomfortable truths — the most valuable SWOT items are often the ones people don't want to discuss
- Stopping at the list without deriving strategy from it
- Not weighting items by importance — a minor strength buried alongside a critical one dilutes the analysis

## When to Combine With Other Frameworks
- **+ Porter's Five Forces**: SWOT gives the company view; Porter's gives the industry view. Together they cover internal and external comprehensively.
- **+ Core Competencies**: Deepens the "Strengths" quadrant — which strengths are truly core and sustainable?
- **+ Business Model Canvas**: SWOT flags issues; BMC maps the system where those issues live.
