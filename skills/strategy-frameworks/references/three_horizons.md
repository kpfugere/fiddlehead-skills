# Three Horizons Model — Reference

## What It Is
McKinsey's framework for balancing **short-term performance with long-term innovation**. Divides initiatives into three time horizons so organizations can sustain today's business while building tomorrow's. Solves the classic "innovator's dilemma" — the tendency to over-invest in the present at the expense of the future.

## The Three Horizons

### Horizon 1: Core Business (Now → 1-3 years)
**The engine.** Current products and services that generate the majority of revenue and profit today.

- **Focus**: Incremental improvements, efficiency, optimization, defending market position
- **Risk level**: Low — proven markets, known customers
- **Resource allocation**: ~70% of innovation effort
- **Metrics**: Revenue, profit margins, market share, cost efficiency, ROI
- **Leadership style**: Execution-focused, operational excellence, process discipline
- **Examples**: Feature updates to existing products, cost reduction programs, geographic expansion of proven offerings

**Danger**: Over-investing here at the expense of H2/H3 leads to stagnation and vulnerability to disruption.

### Horizon 2: Emerging Opportunities (2-5 years)
**The bridge.** Emerging businesses that extend the core into adjacent markets, new segments, or new business models.

- **Focus**: Scaling promising ideas, adjacent expansion, new product lines, new channels
- **Risk level**: Moderate — builds on existing strengths but enters new territory
- **Resource allocation**: ~20% of innovation effort
- **Metrics**: Growth metrics, user adoption, market traction, milestones/validation gates
- **Leadership style**: Agile, adaptive, autonomous teams with executive air cover
- **Examples**: New product line leveraging existing tech, expansion into adjacent market, strategic acquisitions

**Challenge**: H2 is the hardest to manage — it's too new for H1 processes but too structured for H3 experimentation. Requires deliberate protection from the gravitational pull of H1.

### Horizon 3: Future Vision (5+ years)
**The seeds.** Visionary ideas, R&D experiments, and moonshots that could become major growth engines in the distant future.

- **Focus**: Exploration, experimentation, radical innovation, creating options
- **Risk level**: High — most ideas will fail, but the ones that succeed can be transformational
- **Resource allocation**: ~10% of innovation effort
- **Metrics**: Learning velocity, number of experiments, option value, technical feasibility
- **Leadership style**: Entrepreneurial, experimental, high tolerance for failure, small autonomous teams
- **Examples**: Fundamental R&D, incubating new business models, exploring emerging technologies, strategic venture investments

**Key insight**: H3 is about creating *options*, not guaranteed returns. Think portfolio of bets, not business cases.

## The 70-20-10 Rule
A common (though not rigid) allocation guideline:
- **70%** of resources → H1 (sustain and optimize)
- **20%** of resources → H2 (scale emerging bets)
- **10%** of resources → H3 (explore and experiment)

This ratio ensures survival today while investing in tomorrow. Adjust based on industry pace of change — fast-moving industries (tech) may need more in H2/H3; stable industries may weight H1 heavier.

## How to Apply It

1. **Inventory current initiatives.** List all major projects, products, and investments. Classify each as H1, H2, or H3 based on time-to-impact and uncertainty level.
2. **Assess the balance.** Is the portfolio overly weighted toward H1? Are there enough H2 bets to sustain growth when H1 products mature? Are there any H3 seeds planted?
3. **Identify gaps.** If H2 is empty, the company faces a "growth cliff" when current products decline. If H3 is empty, long-term reinvention capacity is at risk.
4. **Design governance for each horizon.** Don't manage H3 like H1 — different metrics, different team structures, different tolerance for failure.
5. **Plan horizon transitions.** Successful H3 experiments should graduate to H2 for scaling. Successful H2 initiatives become H1. This pipeline is what sustains long-term growth.
6. **Protect the long horizons.** H1 will naturally absorb resources because it has immediate ROI. Leadership must actively protect H2/H3 budgets from being raided.

## Common Pitfalls
- **H1 gravity**: The urgency of today's business pulls all attention and resources, starving H2/H3
- **Managing all horizons the same way**: H3 needs experimentation and failure tolerance; applying H1 metrics (ROI, quarterly targets) to H3 kills innovation
- **Treating horizons as purely time-based**: They're also about uncertainty level. An H3 idea might reach market in 2 years if the technology accelerates
- **No graduation path**: H2/H3 initiatives that succeed need a clear path to become core businesses, including resource ramp-up and organizational integration

## When to Combine With Other Frameworks
- **+ BCG Matrix**: BCG shows current portfolio state (Stars, Cows, etc.); Three Horizons maps the time dimension for future planning. Cash Cows fund H2/H3 bets.
- **+ Core Competencies**: Which competencies enable H1? Which need development for H2/H3?
- **+ Blue Ocean**: H2/H3 initiatives are often blue ocean plays — creating new market space.
- **+ Golden Circle**: The Why should be the constant thread across all three horizons.
