# Blue Ocean Strategy — Reference

## What It Is
A framework for escaping cutthroat competition ("red oceans") by creating **uncontested market space** ("blue oceans"). Instead of fighting for existing demand, create new demand through **value innovation** — simultaneously increasing buyer value and reducing costs. Developed by W. Chan Kim and Renée Mauborgne.

## Core Concepts

### Value Innovation (The Cornerstone)
Break the traditional tradeoff between differentiation and low cost. Do both at once:
- **Eliminate** factors the industry takes for granted but customers don't value
- **Reduce** factors well below the industry standard
- **Raise** factors well above the industry standard
- **Create** factors the industry has never offered

This is NOT about technology innovation for its own sake. It's about redefining what value means to the buyer.

### The Strategy Canvas
A diagnostic graph that plots **competitive factors** (x-axis) against **offering level** (y-axis) for each player in the industry. Each competitor gets a "value curve."

**How to use it:**
1. Identify the 6-12 key factors the industry competes on (price, features, service levels, etc.)
2. Score each major competitor on each factor (low-medium-high)
3. Draw the curves — you'll typically see competitors converging on the same profile
4. Design a NEW value curve that dramatically diverges — this is your blue ocean move

*Example: [yellow tail] wine saw all wineries competing on complexity, aging, vineyard prestige. They eliminated those factors and created fun, easy-to-drink wines — diverging completely.*

### Four Actions Framework (ERRC Grid)
The operational tool for designing your new value curve:

| **Eliminate** | **Reduce** |
|---|---|
| Which factors the industry takes for granted should be eliminated entirely? | Which factors should be reduced well below the industry standard? |
| **Raise** | **Create** |
| Which factors should be raised well above the industry standard? | Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered? |

Fill this grid to identify specific moves. The Eliminate and Reduce cells drive cost savings. The Raise and Create cells drive differentiation.

### Three Tiers of Noncustomers
Blue oceans often come from converting noncustomers:
- **Tier 1**: "Soon-to-be" noncustomers sitting on the edge of your market, ready to leave
- **Tier 2**: "Refusing" noncustomers who consciously reject your industry's offerings
- **Tier 3**: "Unexplored" noncustomers in distant markets who've never considered your industry

Looking at noncustomers (especially Tier 2-3) reveals latent demand that competitors ignore.

## How to Apply It

1. **Map the current red ocean.** Draw the strategy canvas for your industry. Where do all competitors converge?
2. **Identify pain points and noncustomers.** What do current customers complain about? Who refuses to buy in this category, and why?
3. **Fill the ERRC Grid.** Be bold — eliminating sacred cows is where the biggest gains come from.
4. **Design the new value curve.** It should look dramatically different from incumbents — focus, divergence, and a compelling tagline.
5. **Validate.** Test with noncustomers. Does the new offering speak to people who previously weren't in the market?
6. **Check the business model.** Value innovation only works if the cost structure supports it. The eliminated/reduced factors must offset the raised/created ones.

## Classic Examples
- **Cirque du Soleil**: Eliminated animals, star performers, multiple rings (circus norms). Created artistic theater, refined venue, thematic storylines. Result: premium pricing, new audience (adults, not kids).
- **Southwest Airlines**: Eliminated meals, assigned seating, hub routing. Created fun culture, point-to-point routes, rapid turnaround. Competed with cars, not other airlines.
- **Nintendo Wii**: Eliminated cutting-edge graphics. Created motion controls, family-friendly casual gaming. Attracted noncustomers (families, elderly) who'd never buy a PlayStation.

## Common Pitfalls
- **The technology trap**: Innovating on tech without connecting to buyer value. Cool tech ≠ blue ocean.
- **Timidity**: Making small tweaks instead of bold divergence. A slightly different value curve is just a red ocean variant.
- **Ignoring execution**: A blue ocean strategy still needs a viable cost structure and business model.
- **Assuming blue oceans last forever**: Competitors eventually follow. Plan for defending the space or finding the next one.

## When to Combine With Other Frameworks
- **+ JTBD**: Reveals the unmet jobs that point to blue ocean opportunities. JTBD tells you *what* to create/raise; Blue Ocean tells you *how* to restructure the offering.
- **+ Porter's Five Forces**: Porter's diagnoses why the red ocean is bloody; Blue Ocean shows the escape route.
- **+ Business Model Canvas**: After designing the ERRC grid, map the new offering onto a BMC to validate operational viability.
